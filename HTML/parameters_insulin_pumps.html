Basal insulin delivery

Background subcutaneous infusion of rapid acting insulin at pre-programmed variable rates over 24 hour periods

Bolus insulin delivery

used to prevent BGL rise with a meal and to correct hyperglycaemia under supplemental/correction dosing

Insulin: carbohydrate ratio [ICR]
  • The insulin:carbohydrate ratio is the number of grams of carbohydrate covered by 1 unit of insulin (or the amount of insulin required to prevent the BG rise due to ingesting that amount of carbohydrate).
  • For example, an ICR of 5 means 1 unit of insulin will cover 5 g of carbohydrate.
  • Some patients with diabetes may instead use the number of units per standard 15g serve (exchange) of carbohydrate. For example, a patient may understand that they require 2 units of insulin per exchange (ie 2 units per 15g carbohydrate serve).
  • Whilst an ‘exchange’ is usually standardised at 15g of carbohydrate, some patients may have been taught to have 10 g carbohydrate exchanges to facilitate calculations by the patient for insulin dosing at mealtimes.
  • It is therefore important to question the patient to clarify the above definitions from their understanding.
Insulin sensitivity factor (ISF)
  • Amount of blood glucose fall per unit of supplemental or correction dose of insulin.
  • For example, ISF 2 means 1 unit of insulin is expected to reduce the blood glucose level by 2 mmol/L over the next 4-5 hours
Target BGL
  • The BGL that the algorithm in pump will attempt to achieve. Target BGL and ISF will determine the amount of insulin given to correct a high blood glucose level
Insulin on board / Active Insulin time
  • term varies with different brands of pump
  • The assumed activity remaining of an administered insulin bolus. It is usually 3-4 hours. 
Total daily dose
  • Total amount of insulin administered over a 24 hour period.
  • Consists of total basal dose and total bolus doses in this period. Ratio of basal to bolus is ideally close to 50:50 assuming an ‘average’ dietary carbohydrate intake.
Emergency insulin regimen

If the pump fails an ‘emergency’ dose should be given, consisting of the total basal dose given as long acting or intermediate insulin, as well pre-meal rapid acting insulin calculated from known carb ratio. If the total basal dose on the pump is not known, calculate the initial basal insulin dose as 0.2x weight (in Kg).
